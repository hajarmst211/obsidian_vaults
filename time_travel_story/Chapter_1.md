He pushed his torso up from the bed. Sunlight had just started to break the darkness of the night, turning the sky into a deep velvet colored with a light orange shade where the sky met the flat ground. A loud, hard huff escaped his throat. In a sudden movement, he got to his feet and lifted his body, taking in the sight of the dusty camp. The morning breeze hit his bare chest and nose first, but it did not seem to penetrate past his first layer of skin.

A few people were out of their cone-shaped tents as well. He went straight to the river to wash his face. He looked at his reflection in the water, decorated by the orange hue of the sky. He watched his reflection, disturbed by the small waves. **He looked like a man haunted by his own ghost; his eyes were hollow pits of failure, framed by the grit of a life spent in the dirt.** In the space of a fleeting heartbeat, he cupped his calloused palms together, plunging them deep into the churning deepness of the stream, and with a desperate fervor, he dashed the icy torrent against his face, as if to scour away the very mark of his own wretchedness. Then, he **shook his head violently** to dry it of water. He cupped his long beard, squeezed the water out of it, and looked at his reflection for a couple more seconds.

"Morning, Yazi," a voice caught him from behind. It startled him. He stood up as quickly as he could and cleared his throat before turning around, but the person was already next to him. That was fast.

"Morning," he said, turning to face him, caught by surprise. "Shero! Is this really you, or am I blind?" He continued, splashing his face with cold river water again.

"Not out of free will, brother," he said, rubbing half of his face with a dry hand. Between every blink, his eyes struggled to stay awake. "I wish I could go back to sleep and have money pour down on me like it does for them," he continued in his dry morning voice.

**That’s what being born on the right side of the wall makes of a person: lazy.** He thought to himself. "You were born on the wrong side of the wall, then," he said, turning back toward the camp.

The road in front of them was flat, earthy, and monotone. One might think the same bunch of dry land and small, icky rocks was regenerating in front of them every ten feet. Only a few red tackers had already taken the road before them, dispersed alone or in groups across the vast domain. Far away on the horizon, the city stood, big, elegant, and shiny. They had five miles left to reach the city, but it looked big enough that they always thought it was just a mile further. Looking at the shiny walls of the city, a red tacker could only wonder how they could devour all the good and beauty inside those walls and leave no tiny bit of it behind. Outside the walls was a place only for red tackers: earthy terrain and a river coming out of the east wall.

"What do you think my master’s wife will be dying of worry about today?" Shero dropped the silence with a wide, smiling voice.

"What was she worrying about yesterday?" Yazi answered, lifting his shoulders.

"What? No… she’s not that boring. Of course, she would find herself a new, useless thing for the day," his words running against each other. "She would probably be thinking all day about what bag would match her dress until sundown, and then she would never leave the house… oh no, no, or maybe she would be thinking about how to make her ugly daughter marry a rich Lord again." They both cracked into laughter.

"My only delight is that I don’t have to encounter such conversations often, brother," Yazi said, catching his breath from the laughs.

As they got closer to the city, the walls grew larger; they could have made fifteen of the biggest tents in the camp. The wooden gates were beginning to welcome people, merchants, and carriages in and out. The sun had already climbed into the sky; the heat was starting to burn, and the city looked more like a refuge from the sun now, although they both knew that wouldn’t be the case.

"They are going to check us again, as if they haven't only let a dozen 'reds' in this year and don't recognize our faces by now," Shero said. "Tsk."

"Just keep your head down, Shero."

At the gate were three soldiers dressed in blue and gold, with gold capes and blue surcoats over their chainmail, big swords at their sides, hands resting on the grips, and open helmets. One of them started coming toward the two friends as he spotted them getting closer. "What are you coming here for, you rats!" he shot into the air between them in an agonizing tone, the tip of his lip crawling up in obvious provocation and **disdain**.

**We would love to kick your smirking face, but unfortunately, today is not that day,** Yazi thought to himself. Instead, he brought up his left arm’s sleeve and turned his wrist to show the red flame tattoo on the **inner forearm**. He looked over his shoulder at Shero and nodded toward the soldier, signaling him to do the same. And so, he did.

The soldier checked both their tattoos. "Yeah, now go on, and you’d better not let me catch you inside after 6:00… you won’t be getting out again if you are that eager to stay," he said again with that same annoying tone as he passed them, letting them in.

**We are not that fond of your shitty-smelling city, neither.** One could swear he was just a fragile boy under his armor. How could wearing shiny armor over their skin make them soldiers and protectors? Yazi swore he could take that silly boy down in one strike.

Before they reached the gates, the roar of the crowd hit their ears, and the scent of flowers, rum, and lust filled their noses. The houses had simple facades of every color, with blossoms upon the steps and two iron lanterns, one on each side of the door, burning with a blue flame that never died, day or night. Yet, the doors were mere portals to a vast interior; gardens, fountains, and chambers draped in silk.

As they walked deeper into the city streets, the people stared, their eyes were determined on making them feel small, low and dirty.

"It is a torture to poison my eyes with this colorful sight every day," Shero complained, his face twisted in disgust. His lips curled as if he had bitten into a sour lemon.

"Bright colors have their charm," a giggle escaped Yazi at the sight of his brother’s sour face.

"No... no... instead of forcing folks to put flowers and useless fires on their doorsteps in the light of day, they ought to forbid any color but black or red."

"I would be so delighted to see you announce this change once you reach the fieries," Yazi tried to hold back his mirth until he got the rise he wanted.

Shero lunged and gave Yazi a sharp smack to the back of his neck. "You take me for a fool, don't you?" His eyes were cold and serious.

Yazi finally burst into laughter. "You take matters too seriously," he said between gasps for air, "flowers and colors only add life to this dying city."

"They are not doing a good job of it," Shero replied, his face hard. His left hand balled into a fist. "Make me the High Fierie, and you would see life erupt from every corner of this city." He flung his hand open, his fingers spread wide as he spoke.

"Aye, aye. This thought would put your head on a spike as quick as an eye flinch ." Yazi’s laughter faded, his voice returning to a steady tone. They reached the crossroads where their paths split toward their labors. "See you at sundown, High Fierie!" He held his hand up, palm curved, waiting for his brother to meet it.

"See you at sundown," Shero replied. He pressed his palm against his brother’s and bumped his shoulder against Yazi’s like a true kin. Then, each went his own way.

The sun had begun its ascent, casting a pale gold light upon the cobblestones’. The blossoms on the doorsteps stirred from their slumber, and the sky was so crystal clear it appeared a mirror of everything innocent in the world . High above, the birds took flight from their nests, their singing sounded a clarion call to the city that a new dawn had broken.

The hum of the morning stirred within Yazi’s veins and his spirit felt a rare feeling of safeness  within the sanctuary of the city walls and not in the wide dusty campsite. Yet, he knew well the eyes of the townsfolk, their gazes were as heavy and sharp as a headsman’s blade, filled with the same ancient prejudice. They would never behold his people for what they truly were, no matter how good and useful the prove themselves to be. He had come to believe that such acceptance was as hard of reach as the top of the wall. His part in this place what clear; labor in silence, complete his tasks correctly, and head back the moment his time was served.

Whilst he stood lost in his morning reflections, he felt a sudden, piercing jab like the keen tip of a spine or a dagger’s point pitch deep into the center of his back. Though the contact lasted nothing but a few seconds, it sent him leaping a full yard forward, his spine arching in reflex and his hand flying to the site of the sting. 

As the sharp agony began to dull, he wheeled about to face his assailant. Standing before him were four city guards, their faces twisted in cruel mirth. Two leaned upon one another, hunched over each other in laughter, while the one at the center fixed Yazi with a smirk. Yazi fixed his posture ignoring the pain, met his gaze with a face as cold as stone. 

"By the Saints," the guard sneered, his voice dripping with scorn after his long silence. "Did you see how he rat leapt? Like a craven hen startled from its coop."

Yazi knew what they pursued, they hungered for his rage, for him breaking. He would not grant them that bounty. He would not feed their broken ego with the satisfaction of his spoil. Nevertheless, agaisnt his will the fire of his range rose from his very heels, yet he kept his gaze locked onto the guard’s eyes, unblinking.

"It seems the mongrel has swallowed his own tongue, brothers!" another guard jeered between his laughs.

Yazi understood that to defend his pride with steel or fist would be the death of him. He turned upon his heel, intent on returning to his quest, but the first man’s voice lashed out at him. "Aye, see him run? A frightened child, hollow of heart and devoid of valor."

*Does he truly believe his gilded breastplate renders him untouchable?* Yazi mused, the urge to silence the man’s insolence warring with his discipline. One blow he could strike him once, and none would dare spit upon his name again. He could reclaim the honor of his people in a single moment of righteous violence. 

Yet, he kept his ground. He recalled his vow, silent and binding as a holy oath. He would walk this path to its end, and he would never give them the satisfaction of winning.
